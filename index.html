Symbols

Hair

Janie’s hair is a significant element in this book, as it is repeatedly focused on throughout the book, from the first scene to the last. The way the hair is portrayed as the story progresses represents her current relationship and lover. In the beginning, when Janie is with Jody, her hair is covered, which is a result of Jody’s controlling attitude, always wanting to exert his authority over Janie. However, this changes when Janie is freed from Jody’s influence. As soon as he dies, Janie begins to wear her hair in a long braid, symbolizing her new-found freedom. Not to mention, her hair is seen as an embodiment of her beauty In addition, her hair has importance to others. “What dat ole forty year ole ’oman doin’ wid her hair swingin’ down her back lak some young gal?”. As you can see from this quote, it is the feature they use to identify Janie when she comes back to town.

Mule

The scene were Jody frees the mule of Matt Bonner can be compared to Janie and Jody’s relationship. Janie is trapped, like the mule, and Jody is the only one with the power to give her freedom. Jody does end up freeing Janie, but through a different method. When he dies, Janie is finally liberated.

Horizon

The horizon is frequently brought up throughout the book, as a vision of a better future. It represents the idealistic view that Janie has, which is shown to be unrealistic in the beginning of the book. However, the true importance of it is that it is what motivates Janie throughout the book, as she chases after it to make it a reality. At the end of the book, what seemed impossible is achieved by Janie.

Music

Similar to literature, music created during the Harlem Renaissance brought light to the African-American experience. Specifically, jazz music gained popularity at this time among both blacks and whites. Many jazz musicians saw this as an opportunity to push black issues in to the public lexicon, and make whites aware of their struggle. One example is Black and Blue by Louis Armstrong, who was one of the greatest jazz musicians of his time. In the lyrics, he sings,

My only sin
Is in my skin
What did I do
To be so black and blue?

Here, he points out how many blacks are discriminated against, and even face violence in the form of lynchings. For many people, especially in the North, this was the first time learning about these issues. Hearing about them through an artist they love helped them realize the problems faced by their African-American counterparts.

Another famous Renaissance song is Strange Fruit by Billie Holliday.

Southern trees bear strange fruit,

Blood on the leaves and blood at the root,

You can see that the song is a metaphorical and extremely unsettling description of a lynching. In this song, the strange fruit is a symbol for the bodies of the lynching victims. By using creepy imagery, Holliday gives listeners a taste of everyday life for blacks living in the south, and how terrible it is.

Poetry

There were many famous black poets during the Harlem Renaissance. One of the most famous was Langston Hughes, who wrote countless poems during this period. One of the most famous is Harlem.

What happens to a dream deferred?

Does it dry up

like a raisin in the sun?

These lines refer to the dreams of African Americans. Their dreams are postponed due to their status in society. He uses imagery which can be considered disgusting, or even violent, to represent what happens to those who dare to dream.
